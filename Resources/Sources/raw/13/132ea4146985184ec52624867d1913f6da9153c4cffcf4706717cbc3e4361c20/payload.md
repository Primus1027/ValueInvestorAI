# How to Evaluate Management: Beyond the Numbers

Buffett has spent sixty-two years serving on twenty-one public company boards, and his conclusion is deceptively simple: the bedrock challenge is to "find and retain a talented CEO — possessing integrity, for sure — who will be devoted to the company for his/her business lifetime." Yet simplicity of statement conceals enormous difficulty of execution. Across decades of shareholder letters, speeches, and acquisition decisions, Buffett, Munger, and their circle have assembled what amounts to a field manual for reading management character — one built not from theory but from billions of dollars in bets, some spectacularly right and a few painfully wrong.

## The Two Yardsticks: Operations and Ownership

Buffett frames management evaluation along two dimensions that, while distinct, tend to correlate:

> "I think you judge management by two yardsticks. One is how well they run the business... And then the second thing you want to figure out is how well that they treat their owners."

The first yardstick — operational performance — must be measured relative to the hand a manager was dealt. Eugene Abegg grew Illinois National Bank from $250,000 of net worth to $17 million over thirty-eight years without outside capital, achieving top-tier returns on deposits and assets. Rose Blumkin built Nebraska Furniture Mart from $72,264 of net worth and fifty dollars of cash into a powerhouse, with no formal schooling and arriving in America speaking no English. These are not stories of inherited advantages; they are stories of owner operator talent compounding over decades with minimal capital.

The second yardstick — shareholder orientation — reveals itself in how management communicates, allocates capital, and structures incentives. Buffett's own partnership terms embodied this: a 4% hurdle rate, 50% carry above the hurdle, no management fee, and personal coverage of 25% of any capital losses. When he and Munger issued Berkshire B-shares near all-time highs, they published an extraordinary disclaimer:

> "Mr. Buffett and Mr. Munger believe that Berkshire's class-A common stock is not under valued at the market price stated above. Neither Mr. Buffett nor Mr. Munger would currently buy Berkshire shares at that price."

This radical honesty — telling prospective buyers not to buy — paradoxically strengthened long-term trust. It also illustrates a deeper principle: management that treats shareholders as partners, not as marks, creates a self-selecting investor base that reinforces good behavior. Berkshire deliberately follows policies that attract long-term, knowledgeable shareholders and discourage speculators, because "if we follow some policies that cause a whole bunch of people to buy Berkshire for the wrong reason, the only way they can buy it is to replace somebody in this room."

Crucially, Buffett insists this evaluation can be done from public filings alone:

> "The conclusions I've come to about managers have really come about the same way you can make yours. I mean they come about by reading reports rather than any intimate personal knowledge."

Comparing a manager's results against competitors and the starting conditions they inherited provides a reliable scorecard. You do not need a private dinner with the CEO. You need annual reports, proxy statements, and competitor filings.

## Integrity as Infrastructure

If operational skill is the engine, integrity is the chassis. Without it, everything else is dangerous. Buffett's acquisition framework demands three attributes — good returns on net tangible capital, able and honest managers, and availability at a sensible price — and honesty is not optional seasoning but a load-bearing pillar.

The National Indemnity acquisition is the canonical example. The entire negotiation took fifteen minutes. The contract was a page and a half, handwritten. Neither company had ever been audited by a public accounting firm, and Buffett did not request one:

> "My reasoning: (1) Jack was honest and (2) He was also a bit quirky and likely to walk away if the deal became at all complicated."

That company now has $111 billion in net worth. Similarly, the NFM acquisition was completed with a 1.25-page proposal, no investment bankers, no lawyers, and unaudited financials — "because Mrs. B's word was sufficient." Trust and character dramatically reduce transaction costs, and the savings compound over decades.

The corollary is equally important: some people, companies, and entire industries are structurally incentivized toward deception. Engaging with them is a losing proposition regardless of apparent opportunity:

> "Some people (and companies or even whole industries) are addicted to 'getting dirty' — deceiving, betraying, evading, cheating, belittling, and generally pursuing their own self-interest above all else. It can be tempting to engage with such people, because they often have or promise great financial rewards. But you can't win in the long run."

Munger extends this into systems design. When incentive structures reward fraud, people learn to be crooked — not because they are intrinsically dishonest but because the system teaches them. "The people who design easily-gameable systems belong in the lowest circle of hell." The mutual fund industry's trillion-dollar fee machine, built on bribing brokers to betray their clients, is Exhibit A.

## The Institutional Imperative: How Good People Make Bad Decisions

Perhaps the most underappreciated concept in management evaluation is the [[institutional_imperative]] — the set of forces within organizations that cause rational individuals to act irrationally. Buffett has documented its pathologies for decades, and the pattern is remarkably consistent.

The CEO role self-selects for action-oriented personalities. Once a chief executive hungers for a deal:

> "He or she will never lack for forecasts that justify the purchase. Subordinates will be cheering, envisioning enlarged domains and the compensation levels that typically increase with corporate size."

Investment bankers amplify the bias, "smelling huge fees." Buffett's advice is blunt: "Don't ask the barber whether you need a haircut." The spreadsheet projections never disappoint, the synergy estimates always pencil, and the fee-driven ecosystem ensures that the voice urging restraint is the quietest in the room.

In insurance, the institutional imperative manifests as the inability to walk away from underpriced business when competitors are writing it. Buffett returns to this theme so frequently — in his 2013, 2014, and 2015 letters, nearly identically — that the repetition itself is instructive:

> "That old line, 'The other guy is doing it, so we must as well,' spells trouble in any business, but in none more so than insurance."

Public pension funds offer a broader illustration. Their institutional imperative to seek "sophisticated" strategies led them into hedge funds that delivered both poor performance and enormous fees — shortfalls that local taxpayers must cover for decades. Wealthy individuals fall into the same trap through a different psychological channel: they feel entitled to exclusive, superior products and cannot accept that a simple index fund available to everyone might outperform their bespoke arrangements.

Munger identifies a subtler form of the imperative: organizations become prisoners of their prior success formulas. Walmart failed to compete with Costco because "they were too weathered by the ideas they already had. That's everybody's trouble. They just can't accept a new idea because the space is occupied by the old idea." Sears lost to Walmart despite decades of accumulated advantages because its bureaucracy punished new thinking: "If you poked your head up with a new thought, the system kind of turned against you."

Even Berkshire is not immune. GEICO fell behind Progressive in data analytics — a capability identified as critical by Todd Combs in 2010 — because Berkshire's decentralized management approach delayed the necessary pivot. The tension between decentralization and oversight is real, and pretending otherwise is itself a form of the institutional imperative.

## Skin in the Game: The Ultimate Alignment Mechanism

The single most reliable signal of management quality is skin in the game. When a manager's personal wealth rises and falls with shareholder outcomes, the need for elaborate monitoring diminishes.

Buffett's letter to Kay Graham articulated this with precision:

> "This purchase represents a sizable commitment to us and an explicitly quantified complement to The Post as a business enterprise, and to you as its Chief Executive. Writing a check separates conviction from the conversation."

Clayton Homes demonstrates the principle at the operational level. By retaining 100% of the mortgages it originates, Clayton creates perfect alignment between originator and lender — the exact alignment that was absent in the originate-to-distribute model that caused the 2008 financial crisis. "When we make mistakes in granting credit, we therefore pay a price — a hefty price that dwarfs any profit we realized upon the original sale of the home."

The absence of skin in the game is equally diagnostic. Munger observes that the 2-and-20 or 3-and-30 fee structure in private funds "attracts people motivated by fee income rather than investment excellence. It's easier to pretend that you can get good results than to actually achieve them." Investment consultants cannot recommend static index funds because "that would be career suicide," so they recommend frequent managerial shifts wrapped in esoteric jargon — regardless of whether changes add value.

Berkshire's internal structure reinforces alignment through an unusual mechanism: no company-wide budget and no quarterly target to hit. This eliminates the pressure that causes managers to manipulate numbers:

> "Our lack of such an instrument means that the parent company has never had a quarterly 'number' to hit. Shunning the use of this bogey sends an important message to our many managers, reinforcing the culture we prize."

Management that regularly highlights "adjusted earnings" to wave away real costs fosters contagion: "CEOs who overtly look for ways to report high numbers tend to foster a culture in which subordinates strive to be 'helpful' as well." The rot starts at the top.

## Owner-Operators vs. Hired Managers: A Critical Distinction

Buffett evolved from a controlling activist into a passive partner of great managers. His ideal acquisition target is a business built by a founder who "retains every bit of the proprietary interest and pride that they have always had" even after selling for cash. The operator's skill is the scarce resource, not the capital.

Ajit Jain exemplifies the hired manager who transcends the category. He created an insurance structure at Berkshire that didn't exist before and can't be easily replicated. Buffett would take Jain's judgment over any AI product for the next decade, "even with $100 billion at stake." When asked about Tim Cook, Buffett made a striking admission: "I'm somewhat embarrassed to say that Tim Cook has made Berkshire a lot more money than I've ever made Berkshire Hathaway."

BYD's Wang Chuanfu represents the founder-operator archetype taken to its extreme. Munger valued him for "combining deep engineering knowledge with hands-on production execution capability" — a rare concentration of talent in one person. When both Munger and Buffett tried to talk him out of entering the car business, "he paid no attention to us and went right ahead." The lesson: when you back a genuine genius, their conviction can override your strategic concerns.

But the owner-operator model has failure modes too. Buffett's "White Knight Acquirer Playbook" of keeping existing management in place backfired catastrophically at Gen Re, resulting in $300 million in fraud losses, billion-dollar Hollywood deal losses, $2 billion in 9/11 losses, and a major AIG accounting scandal — all because he was "strictly hands off" with management that did not deserve such trust. Tom Murphy's maxim cuts both ways: "To achieve a reputation as a good manager, just be sure you buy good businesses." If the business or manager is flawed, hands-off stewardship becomes hands-off negligence.

Munger and Buffett's approach with Wesco Financial reveals the nuance. They deliberately paid above-market prices to maintain goodwill with management, understanding that "Louis Vincente doesn't really need to work for us. If he felt that we were slobs or something, it just wouldn't work." Retaining talented operators who built great businesses requires not extracting every last dollar of value in negotiations.

## The Information Cocoon: How Leaders Destroy Their Own Judgment

Munger's most penetrating observation about management failure concerns the suppression of bad news. People develop automatic antipathy toward those who deliver unpleasant truths:

> "He didn't like to hear what he didn't like to hear. And people soon learned that. So they told Paley only what he liked to hear. Therefore, he was soon living in a little cocoon of unreality and everything else was corrupt — although it was a great business."

This is not merely a personality flaw; it is a structural risk. Any leader who punishes bearers of bad news creates an information cocoon where reality is filtered out. Capital allocation decisions made inside such cocoons — like Westinghouse's billions in bad real estate loans, or P&G moving "from selling soap to writing puts on bonds" — carry hidden risks that surface only after enormous damage is done.

The antidote is what Buffett describes as intellectual humility calibrated to reality:

> "It's great to have a manager with a 160 IQ — unless he thinks it's 180."

Overestimating one's intelligence is more dangerous than having less of it. The manager who accurately knows the boundaries of their competence will delegate wisely, seek disconfirming evidence, and avoid the information cocoon. The one who believes their brilliance extends everywhere will surround themselves with yes-men and drift into catastrophe.

Buffett's own succession planning reflects this understanding. His honest acknowledgment of declining energy created space for Greg Abel to genuinely take over operations. The operating executives "overwhelmingly prefer to talk to Greg or to Ajit" — not because Buffett is disrespected, but because he redirected callers gracefully and recognized that the managers actually wanted more direction than he had been providing.
