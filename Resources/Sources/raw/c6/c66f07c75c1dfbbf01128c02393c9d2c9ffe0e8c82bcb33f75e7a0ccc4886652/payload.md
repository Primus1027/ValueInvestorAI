# Berkshire's Insurance Float: The Engine of Compounding

Few financial innovations have generated as much lasting wealth as Warren Buffett's transformation of insurance float into a perpetual compounding engine. What began with a $17 million premium base in 1967 grew into $176 billion of investable capital by 2025 — capital that, through extraordinary underwriting discipline, has cost Berkshire less than nothing over its lifetime. This is the story of how a commodity business became the most powerful capital structure in modern finance.

## The Mechanics: Why Float Is the Perfect Liability

Property-casualty insurance operates on a deceptively simple premise: collect premiums today, pay claims tomorrow. But the temporal gap between collection and payout creates something remarkable — a pool of other people's money that the insurer can invest for its own benefit. Buffett has spent decades explaining this mechanism with increasing precision, and the language he uses reveals how deeply he thinks about its structural properties.

> "this is a loan that someone is making you at 0% interest. You're like, well, that's a pretty good loan. I don't have to service the debt. That means that I basically can make more profits, because I don't have to take a cut of my profits every month to pay down the debt"

But float is not merely a zero-interest loan. It possesses a quality that no conventional debt instrument can match: diversification of the liability base across thousands of independent counterparties. A bank can face a run. A bondholder can demand repayment. But insurance float derives from a distributed portfolio of policyholders whose individual decisions are uncorrelated.

> "it's not one person that loaned me money. It's a gigantic set of thousands, or tens of thousands, or hundreds of thousands of people that are paying me money. Well, then what that means is they're predictable because that's not just somebody wakes up on the wrong side of the bed and says that they want their money back"

This structural stability gives float a durability that transforms its economic character. GAAP accounting deducts float as a full liability, but Buffett has argued persistently — across shareholder letters from 2013 through 2024 — that this treatment dramatically overstates the true economic obligation:

> "Owing $1 that in effect will never leave the premises — because new business is almost certain to deliver a substitute — is worlds different from owing $1 that will go out the door tomorrow and not be replaced."

The revolving-fund metaphor is central. As long as the insurance business continues writing policies, outgoing claims are continuously replaced by incoming premiums. The aggregate balance persists even as individual policies turn over. This is why Buffett calls float "sticky" and why he can invest it with a long term orientation that competitors — constrained by shorter-duration liabilities or capital redemption risk — cannot match.

> "Funds attributable to our insurance operations come and go daily, but their aggregate total is immune from precipitous decline. When it comes to investing float, we can therefore think long-term."

By 2022, Berkshire's float had grown 8,000-fold from its 1967 starting point. The $64 billion increase in float between 2000 and 2016 alone appeared nowhere on Berkshire's balance sheet as an asset. This is the "huge reason," as Buffett put it, that [[intrinsic_value]] far exceeds book value — the largest such gap of any Berkshire business segment.

## The Discipline: Underwriting as Old Testament Religion

The beauty of the float model contains a fatal trap. Because premiums arrive as cash before any claims are known, the business feels enormously profitable in the short run regardless of whether policies are priced correctly. This cash-first dynamic attracts the undisciplined.

> "Insurance always looks easier than it is, and it's so much fun because you get the money at the start, you know, and then you find out whether you've done something stupid later on."

The reinsurance market amplifies this problem because the delay between writing a policy and discovering its true cost can stretch over years or decades. Buffett invokes Munger's swimming metaphor to describe the reckoning:

> "You don't find out who's been swimming naked until the tide goes out. Essentially that's what happens in reinsurance. You don't, you really don't find out who's been swimming naked until the wind blows at them."

GEICO's near-death experience in the 1970s illustrates the compounding danger of mispriced risk. When GEICO expanded from its low-risk government-employee base to the general population without repricing, the damage was not immediately visible. But insurance has a vicious asymmetry: you cannot retroactively raise premiums on policies already written.

> "Once you realize that you're going to be on the hook for a lot more dollars than you have capital available, you're in for a long period of pain because the premiums that you got are already in the bank. You can't go get more money from these customers."

The lesson Buffett drew — "there is never any such thing as a bad risk, but there is such a thing as a bad price" — became the intellectual foundation of Berkshire's underwriting discipline. Jack Ringwalt, whose National Indemnity was Berkshire's first insurance acquisition, embodied this principle from 1941 onward:

> "Mr. Ringwalt has held to the principle of underwriting for a profit — a policy which is frequently talked about within the industry but much less frequently achieved"

At Berkshire, this discipline is not a quarterly aspiration but what Buffett calls "a religion, Old Testament style." The practical manifestation is willingness to shrink. When pricing deteriorates, Berkshire writes less business rather than chasing volume to maintain market presence. This sounds simple, but it requires resisting enormous institutional pressure during soft markets — pressure that Buffett acknowledges can persist for years:

> "Unfortunately, there is a lag between deterioration of underwriting results and tempering of competition. During this period we expect to continue to have negative volume comparisons in our traditional operation."

The payoff for this discipline is staggering. Berkshire's float grew from $46 billion to $171 billion over two decades while simultaneously generating $32 billion in after-tax underwriting profits. The float was not merely free — Berkshire was paid to hold it. As of 2025, GEICO alone achieved sub-80 combined ratios under Todd Combs, translating to the largest underwriting profit in personal auto insurance. Buffett's $50 million investment in half of GEICO now generates $29 billion in float plus $1 billion in quarterly underwriting profit — a return that defies conventional financial mathematics.

## The Builders: GEICO, General Re, and Ajit Jain

Berkshire's insurance empire rests on three pillars, each contributing different qualities to the float engine.

**GEICO** provides scale and structural [[competitive_moat]]. Its direct-to-consumer model eliminates agent commissions, creating a permanent cost advantage over competitors. Lower costs mean competitive premiums that attract more customers, generating more float, in a self-reinforcing cycle. Combs's recent transformation — catching up on telematics, improving rate-to-risk matching, and reducing headcount from 50,000 to 30,000, saving $2 billion annually — demonstrates that a 90-year-old product can still be radically improved through better pricing intelligence.

**General Re** is the cautionary tale within the empire. Its acquisition brought massive float but also a culture of sloppy underwriting. The catastrophic 9/11 losses were not from excessive risk-taking but from something worse — uncompensated risk, policies with "holes" where Gen Re bore exposure without adequate premium. This is a critical distinction in [[risk_management]]: the problem was not the size of the bet but the price of the ticket.

**Ajit Jain** represents the apex of insurance as intellectual craft. His reinsurance operation deliberately seeks out the largest, most exotic risks that traditional insurers refuse to underwrite — premiums exceeding $1 million per policy. His competitive edge is not risk appetite but pricing accuracy: the ability to correctly value one-of-a-kind exposures that defy actuarial tables.

> "There's probably never been anybody better than Ajit at pricing insurance. He is also hyper aggressive."

The division of labor between Ajit and Buffett captures Berkshire's structural advantage in miniature: Ajit generates float at zero or negative cost; Warren deploys it into productive assets. Two distinct skills, combined into a single capital engine.

> "So it's Ajit's job to develop a lot of money for us at no cost or less, and then he hands it to me, and he says, 'You do something with it, Warren.'"

## Float as Leverage — and Why It Beats Debt

Buffett has been philosophically opposed to conventional leverage throughout his career, for reasons Munger articulated with characteristic bluntness: "Do not live your life in such a fashion that a bad day can kill you." The expected-value argument for leverage — that it boosts returns in most scenarios — ignores the catastrophic downside in the tail:

> "Rational people don't risk what they have and need for what they don't have and don't need."

Yet Berkshire is one of the most leveraged large companies in America. The reconciliation of this apparent contradiction is that float is a fundamentally different form of leverage. Unlike bank debt, it cannot be called. Unlike bond debt, it carries no coupon. Unlike deposits, it faces no risk of runs. And when underwriting is profitable, it carries a negative cost — the company is paid to use it.

> "So far, those funds, though they are recorded as a huge net liability on our balance sheet, have been of more utility to us than an equivalent amount of equity. That's because they have usually been accompanied by underwriting earnings."

Berkshire's B-share issuance in 1996 reveals how Buffett thinks about the capital hierarchy. He described the permanent equity raised as "even better than float. You never have to get the cash back." This ordering — equity above float above debt — inverts the traditional corporate finance preference for cheap debt over expensive equity. For Buffett, the permanence and unconditional nature of capital matters more than its stated cost.

Munger acknowledged the opportunity cost of this conservatism. Berkshire "could have been worth twice as much" with modest additional leverage and minimal extra risk. But the duty to shareholders — many of whom had concentrated their savings in Berkshire — overrode personal risk tolerance. This is [[margin_of_safety]] applied not to security analysis but to capital structure.

The contrast with private equity's approach to insurance is instructive. PE firms entering the insurance market take on higher leverage and credit risk, substituting conservative investments for higher-yielding assets. Buffett's warning is pointed: these strategies work during good economies but create systemic risk for policyholders — exactly the kind of risk that Berkshire's conservative approach is designed to survive.

## The Countercyclical Advantage

Berkshire's most underappreciated strategic insight is its deliberate countercyclical positioning. While competitors expand during soft markets (when pricing is inadequate) and retreat after catastrophes (when pricing improves), Berkshire does the opposite:

> "When catastrophes occur and underwriting experience sours, we plan to have the resources available to handle the increasing volume which we will then expect to be available at proper prices."

This requires maintaining enormous capital reserves during periods when that capital earns below-market returns — an opportunity cost that most managers and boards refuse to bear. But the payoff comes in moments of market dislocation, when Berkshire's willingness and capacity to absorb extreme losses creates a structural advantage no competitor can match.

> "No private insurer has the willingness to take on the amount of risk that Berkshire can provide. At times, this advantage can be important."

The Florida market illustrates the pattern. When others retreated from hurricane exposure, Berkshire increased its Florida book when pricing turned favorable and "fortunately nothing bad happened. So a lot of our premiums that were in the top line flew straight to the bottom line." This is the insurance equivalent of Buffett's famous counsel to be greedy when others are fearful — but executed with underwriting precision rather than investment intuition.

A subtler advantage emerges from inflation. Because policies are repriced annually, rising costs translate promptly into larger premiums, higher coverage amounts, and bigger float pools. Insurance is a natural inflation hedge, and Buffett points out the paradox: GEICO would be doing $600 million in annual business instead of $23 billion if loss costs had remained unchanged. The very forces that erode purchasing power elsewhere enlarge the insurance franchise.

## The Unrecognized Asset

Perhaps the deepest insight in Buffett's decades of writing about float is the systematic disconnect between accounting treatment and economic reality. GAAP records float as a dollar-for-dollar liability. But if the float is costless, permanent, and growing, its true economic burden is a fraction of its face value. This means Berkshire's balance sheet structurally understates the company's worth — not by a small amount, but by tens of billions of dollars.

Share repurchases amplify this effect through a mechanism few investors notice. When Berkshire buys back shares, float per remaining share increases automatically. Between 2019 and 2021, float per A-share grew 25% from $79,387 to $99,497. Each remaining share commands a larger portion of a growing, costless capital base — a [[compounding]] effect invisible in standard financial metrics.

After 57 years of building this operation, Buffett's summary is characteristically understated: "We have much room to grow." The competitive advantages — Ajit's pricing genius, GEICO's cost structure, Berkshire's unmatched capacity for catastrophic risk, the discipline to walk away from bad pricing — are not replicable by competitors starting from scratch. The float engine, once built, generates its own fuel.

> "Berkshire's great managers, premier financial strength and a variety of business models protected by wide moats amount to something unique in the insurance world. This assemblage of strengths is a huge asset for Berkshire shareholders that will only get more valuable with time."
