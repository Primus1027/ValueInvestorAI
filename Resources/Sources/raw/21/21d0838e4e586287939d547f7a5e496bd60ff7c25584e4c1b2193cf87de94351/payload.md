# Warren Buffett

The most studied investor in history. Chairman and CEO of Berkshire Hathaway since 1965. Grew a failing New England textile mill into a $1T+ conglomerate by following two rules: buy great businesses at fair prices, and let compounding do the work.

**Born**: 1930, Omaha, Nebraska
**Works referenced in this wiki**: 32 primary sources
**Total extracted insights**: 7,114
**Core partner**: [[charlie_munger]]

## Thought Evolution

Buffett's approach has not been static. He reinvented his strategy at least four times, each reinvention triggered by a concrete lesson from his own mistakes or from Charlie Munger's influence.

**Phase 1 — Cigar Butts (1950-1968)**
Trained by [[benjamin_graham]] at Columbia, Buffett bought quantitatively cheap stocks trading below liquidation value. The partnership era. Works: Sanborn Map, Dempster Mill, Berkshire Hathaway itself. Key source: [[sources/buffett_partnership_letters_1957_70|Buffett Partnership Letters (1957-70)]].

**Phase 2 — Quality at Fair Price (1969-1989)**
Munger's influence: "Forget what you know about buying fair companies at wonderful prices. Instead, buy wonderful companies at fair prices." See's Candies (1972) was the turning point — Buffett paid 3x book value, a price Graham would never have endorsed, and it worked. Key sources: the 1972-1995 shareholder letters.

**Phase 3 — Concentrated Megabets (1988-2016)**
Coca-Cola (1988), American Express (1991), Wells Fargo (1990), Burlington Northern (2009). Buffett moved from dozens of positions to a handful of high-conviction bets, each sized at 5-20% of portfolio. Key source: [[sources/berkshire_hathaway_letters_to_shareholders|Berkshire Hathaway Letters to Shareholders]].

**Phase 4 — Scale Problem (2016-present)**
At $800B+ market cap, Buffett acknowledged that only deals "big enough to move the needle" matter. Apple (2016) became the single largest position in Berkshire history at ~$150B cost basis, proving he could adapt even in his 80s. Key sources: the 2016-2025 shareholder letters and [[sources/cnbc_buffett_annual_squawk_box_interview_feb_2018|Squawk Box interviews]].

## Notable Mistakes and Corrections

Buffett is unusually public about his errors. The important ones are documented in his shareholder letters; each became a frame for his next phase.

**Buying Berkshire Hathaway itself (1965)**
Bought a dying textile mill out of spite after CEO Seabury Stanton reneged on a tender price. Estimated cost: $200B+ in opportunity cost over the lifetime, because the capital tied up in textiles could not compound elsewhere. Called it his "dumbest stock" publicly. See [[mistakes_and_lessons]].

**Selling GEICO too early (1976)**
Sold his partnership-era GEICO stake for a small profit; the position would have been worth hundreds of millions more if held. Corrected decisively in 1996 when he bought all of GEICO.

**Passing on Walmart (1970s-80s)**
Famously couldn't bring himself to buy Walmart shares at a valuation he considered slightly rich. Cost estimate: $10B+.

**Passing on Google and Amazon (1999-2010s)**
"I blew it on Google" — Buffett's own words. Did not see the moat until too late. Partially corrected by Apple (2016), which he now treats as a consumer-products business with a technology moat.

**Buying Dexter Shoe with Berkshire stock (1993)**
Paid $433M in stock for a shoe company that went to zero. Because he paid in Berkshire shares, the opportunity cost compounded — he estimates the real cost at $7-9B.

**The ConocoPhillips oil-peak purchase (2008)**
Bought heavily at the top of the oil cycle, then had to write down the position. Cited as a "commodity business, wrong timing" mistake.

## High-Frequency Themes

Themes that recur across nearly every annual letter and interview.

**Intrinsic value vs book value divergence** — Buffett insists that book value understates Berkshire's worth and walks through the reconciliation in almost every letter. See [[intrinsic_value]].

**The Berkshire model vs conventional holding companies** — Radical decentralization, 25-person HQ, no inter-division capital allocation by committee. See [[berkshire_organizational_model]].

**Insurance float as leverage without recourse** — The cornerstone of Berkshire's compound rate. Premiums come in today, claims paid out years later, and in the interim the capital is Buffett's to invest. See [[insurance_float_machine]].

**Capital allocation as the CEO's job #1** — Most CEOs come up through operations, sales, or engineering and have never allocated capital before running the company. See [[capital_allocation_ceo_most_important_job]].

**Avoiding businesses with capital-intensive moats that leak** — Airlines, textiles, telecoms. Businesses where you have to keep re-investing to stay in place.

**Circle of competence is about honesty, not size** — Repeated almost verbatim in a dozen sources: "the size of the circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries is vital."



**The 2008 crisis as a teaching moment** -- Munger's [[sources/charlie_munger_caltech_dubridge_lecture_2008|Caltech DuBridge Lecture (2008)]] captured both Buffett and Munger's real-time thinking during the crisis, including the Goldman Sachs and GE investments.
