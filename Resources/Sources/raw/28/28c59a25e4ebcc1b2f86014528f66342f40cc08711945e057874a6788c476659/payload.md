# Intrinsic Value: Theory and Practice

The concept of intrinsic value sits at the center of every serious investment decision, yet it resists the precise quantification that modern finance craves. Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger have spent six decades refining an approach that treats valuation not as a spreadsheet exercise but as a judgment about economic reality — one that deliberately sacrifices precision in favor of accuracy. Their framework, rooted in discounted cash flow reasoning but hostile to false exactitude, offers a counterpoint to both the mechanical models of Wall Street and the nihilism of efficient market hypothesis proponents who deny that intrinsic value can be estimated at all.

## The Concept Behind DCF

Discounted cash flow analysis, properly understood, is not a formula — it is a way of thinking. The core proposition is elemental: a business is worth the present value of all cash it will generate for its owners from now until judgment day. Every asset Buffett has purchased, from a tenant farm at age fifteen to Coca-Cola at age fifty-eight, has been evaluated through this single lens.

The tenant farm made the logic tangible. Buffett split crop profits fifty-fifty with the farmer who worked the land — a direct claim on real, recurring cash flows from a productive asset. This was not speculation on what someone else might pay; it was ownership of an earnings stream. That same instinct guided him decades later when he assessed whether Coca-Cola's brand durability justified its price, or whether Japanese trading companies offered compelling value relative to their distributable cash.

> "If we think the present value of the future earning power is attractive enough compared to the purchase price, we would not be overwhelmed by what the first year's figures would look like."

This statement captures something essential: DCF thinking liberates the investor from the tyranny of current-year earnings. A business generating zero cash today may possess tremendous intrinsic value if its future earning power is large enough and probable enough. Conversely, a business throwing off substantial cash today may be worth very little if that cash must be endlessly reinvested merely to maintain competitive position — the lesson Munger absorbed from watching a Caterpillar tractor dealer watch his profits rust in the yard.

> "He looks at his used machines, takes it in as customers buy the new ones and says, 'There's all my profit, rusting in my yard.' We hate that kind of business."

The discount rate itself reflects judgment, not formula. Buffett has disclosed using at least ten percent for after-tax cash streams, adjusted downward for businesses with higher certainty. "The more certain we feel about a business, the closer we are willing to play it." This is the opposite of academic capital asset pricing model methodology, which assigns discount rates based on historical price volatility — a measure Buffett and Munger consider irrelevant to actual investment risk.

## Owner Earnings vs. the Accounting Mirage

If DCF is the conceptual framework, owner earnings is the practical unit of measurement. Reported earnings under GAAP are a crude starting point, but they systematically misrepresent economic reality in both directions.

The distortions compound across Berkshire's structure. Of the $4.4 billion Berkshire's share of its Big Four holdings earned in one representative year, only $1.4 billion appeared in reported results. The retained $3 billion — invisible to anyone reading Berkshire's income statement — compounded silently through reinvestment and share buybacks.

> "Make no mistake: The $3 billion of their earnings we don't report is every bit as valuable to us as the portion Berkshire records."

Buffett repeated this point across multiple annual letters with nearly identical language because the misunderstanding persists. Investors anchored to reported dividends systematically undervalue companies that retain and reinvest earnings at high rates of return. The look-through earnings concept — counting your proportional share of an investee's total earnings, not just the dividends received — corrects this blindness.

On the other side, GAAP accounting creates an asymmetry in how Berkshire's owned businesses are carried on the books. Acquisitions that deteriorate get written down, but those that appreciate in value are never written up. Over decades, this one-way ratchet caused book value to become an increasingly conservative — and ultimately misleading — proxy for intrinsic value. Buffett's willingness to repurchase shares at 120% of book value reflected his knowledge that the true gap had grown enormous.

> "Over time, this asymmetrical accounting treatment necessarily widens the gap between intrinsic value and book value."

The 2018 accounting rule change requiring unrealized investment gains and losses to flow through net income made GAAP bottom-line figures, in Buffett's words, "useless" for analytical purposes. The lesson generalizes: accounting is indispensable as a starting language, but the investor's job is translation.

## The EBITDA Delusion

No single metric better illustrates the gap between accounting convenience and economic truth than EBITDA. By adding back depreciation and amortization to earnings, EBITDA pretends that capital expenditures are optional — that the wear and decay of productive assets is a mere accounting fiction rather than a real economic cost.

Berkshire's textile operations provided six years of empirical evidence against this fantasy. From 1961 to 1966, the business generated essentially zero net earnings — $40,476 total — while requiring heavy capital expenditures merely to remain competitive. The machinery wore out whether or not the accountants chose to depreciate it aggressively. A business that appears profitable on an EBITDA basis but consumes all its cash through mandatory reinvestment is not profitable at all; it is a capital trap.

Munger was characteristically blunt about the limitations of accounting precision more broadly:

> "You have to know enough about it to understand its limitations — because although accounting is the starting place, it's only a crude approximation."

The depreciation schedules that generate EBITDA's denominator are themselves rough guesses. But the solution to imprecise depreciation is not to ignore depreciation entirely — it is to think carefully about actual maintenance capex versus growth capex, and to recognize that only the cash remaining after all necessary reinvestment belongs to the owner.

## Why Precision Is the Enemy of Accuracy

A McKinsey partner once advised the Washington Post Company against repurchasing its own stock when it traded at one-fifth of what Munger called "what an orangutan could figure was the plain value per share by just counting up the values and dividing by the number of shares outstanding." The consultant's reasoning followed efficient market theory: if the stock traded at that price, the price must be right.

> "Washington Post stock was selling at a fifth of what an orangutan could figure was the plain value per share by just counting up the values and dividing by the number of shares."

The Post did buy back its shares, enriching remaining shareholders by over a billion dollars. The episode illustrates a recurring pathology: the pursuit of analytical precision — elaborate models, precise discount rates, quarterly earnings forecasts to the penny — crowds out the rough but accurate judgments that actually generate returns.

Buffett's approach to the $6 million business opportunity crystallizes this philosophy. The business had $2 million in cash, $2 million in real estate, and earned $2 million pre-tax annually. The correct response was immediate action, not extended due diligence.

> "The trick when you get in business with somebody who wants to sell you something for $6 million that's got $2 million of cash, a couple million of real estate, and earned $2 million pre-tax annually — the right response was immediate action."

This is the two-minute valuation in practice. When the gap between price and value is enormous, no amount of additional analysis improves the decision. The analytical effort should be proportional to the closeness of the call. Buffett's decomposition of value into probability times magnitude — willingly paying up for high-probability, moderate-magnitude outcomes while avoiding low-probability, high-magnitude bets — is itself a framework for knowing when precision matters and when it does not.

## Growth as a Component of Value

Growth and value are not opposing philosophies; growth is simply one variable in the value equation. A dollar of earnings from a business that can reinvest at high returns is worth more than a dollar from a business with no reinvestment opportunities. The question is always whether growth creates or destroys value — whether incremental capital earns above its cost.

Berkshire's insurance float illustrates value-creating growth at its purest. Float grew from $20 million to nearly $3 billion at costs so low they sometimes turned negative through underwriting profits. Each increment of float was essentially free capital deployed into Buffett's investment portfolio, compounding the value of the enterprise in ways that no static earnings multiple could capture.

Yet growth can equally destroy value. The textile business grew its revenues through capital-intensive expansion that earned nothing for shareholders across full economic cycles. The pari-mutuel insight applies: a growth company priced for perfection may deliver worse returns than a stagnant business purchased at a sufficient discount.

> "Any damn fool could plainly see that IBM had better business prospects than the railroad. But once you put the price into the formula, it wasn't so clear."

The Munger evolution — from cigar butt investing to acquiring wonderful businesses at fair prices — did not abandon price discipline. It refined the calculus to account for the compounding power of businesses with durable competitive advantage, recognizing that a business able to reinvest retained earnings at twenty percent returns deserves a materially higher multiple than one that must distribute everything to avoid value destruction.

## Preserving the Right to Be Right

The ultimate test of any valuation framework is not the accuracy of the estimate but the survival of the estimator. Intrinsic value analysis is worthless if the investor is forced to liquidate before price converges with value — a lesson the Great Depression taught with extraordinary brutality.

> "What was unique and remarkable about the Great Depression was that even the smart money got wiped out. The people who realized things are cheap now... were destroyed."

Buffett's insistence on permanent financial conservatism — maintaining at least $20 billion in cash equivalents, avoiding material short-term debt, never leveraging to the point where a severe downturn could force liquidation — is not timidity. It is the recognition that the difference between earning slightly more or less is acceptable, but the difference between earning something and going broke is an equation that does not work.

> "Whether you earn x or go broke is not an equation that works. They didn't give it to us to lose it."

Munger acknowledged that modest leverage could have tripled Berkshire's returns without catastrophic risk, but both partners refused to give the "slightest chance of screwing up our basic shelter position." This is [[margin_of_safety]] applied not to a single investment but to the enterprise itself — ensuring the capacity to act when, roughly once per decade, dark clouds fill the economic skies and briefly rain gold.

The practical implication is that intrinsic value estimation must always be embedded within a survival framework. The investor who is approximately right about value but certainly solvent will outperform the investor who is precisely right about value but occasionally bankrupt. Precision is the enemy of accuracy, and accuracy is the servant of survival.
