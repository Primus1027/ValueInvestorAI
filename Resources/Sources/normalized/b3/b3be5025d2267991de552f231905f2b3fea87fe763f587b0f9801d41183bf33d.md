# Learning from Mistakes: Buffett and Munger's Biggest Errors

<!-- p:4837f0 -->
Warren Buffett has called his 2013 shareholder letter a "confession" and his annual meetings a recurring exercise in intellectual honesty. Charlie Munger went further, arguing that studying failure is more instructive than studying success. Together they built one of history's greatest investment records not by avoiding all mistakes — they made plenty — but by extracting maximum learning from each one and, crucially, by never allowing a single error to become fatal. This synthesis examines their most consequential mistakes, the structural lessons each yielded, and the deeper asymmetry between learning from failure and learning from success.

<!-- p:64534c -->
## The Commission Errors: Dexter Shoe, US Air, and Tesco

<!-- p:29d864 -->
The most painful mistakes in the Berkshire record are not the ones that lost money in absolute terms but the ones that destroyed the most value per unit of avoidable stupidity — Buffett's own framing. Three investments stand out.

<!-- p:6a45e1 -->
**Dexter Shoe** (1993) was a New England shoe manufacturer Buffett acquired for $433 million in Berkshire stock. Within a few years, cheap foreign imports obliterated its competitive position, and the business went to zero. The error was twofold: Buffett misjudged the durability of Dexter's moat, and he compounded the mistake by paying in Berkshire shares rather than cash. The stock he surrendered would eventually be worth billions. Dexter illustrates a principle Munger articulated at USC in 1994: every investor must honestly assess their [[circle_of_competence]], and the penalty for straying outside it is not a modest haircut but potential total loss.

<!-- p:678f45 -->
> "You have to figure out what your own aptitudes are. If you play games where other people have the aptitudes and you don't, you're going to lose."

<!-- p:48e3a4 -->
**US Air** (1989) was a preferred stock investment that nearly went to zero when airline industry economics deteriorated. Buffett later described it as a case where he let the attractiveness of the terms — a high preferred dividend — seduce him into a business he knew had terrible fundamental economics. The lesson reinforced what would become a Berkshire axiom: the quality of the business always dominates the terms of the deal. A wonderful price for a terrible business is still a terrible investment.

<!-- p:e96d7b -->
**Tesco** (2013–2014) was a UK grocery chain where Berkshire held a significant position. When accounting irregularities emerged, Buffett hesitated to sell, compounding the loss. He later called it a mistake of both commission and inaction — he should have recognized the deterioration earlier, and once he did, he should have exited immediately rather than hoping for recovery. The Tesco experience echoes Munger's Baltimore department store lesson:

<!-- p:f697d1 -->
> "So we decided just to reverse it and take the hits to look foolish rather than go broke."

<!-- p:9ff28f -->
The willingness to take a loss and look foolish is a form of intellectual honesty that most investors struggle with. Loss aversion and commitment bias conspire to keep people in deteriorating positions long after the original thesis has broken.

<!-- p:c5e2c2 -->
## The Errors of Omission: Walmart, Google, and the Roads Not Taken

<!-- p:2a3b94 -->
Buffett has repeatedly said that his worst mistakes are not the investments he made but the ones he failed to make. He understood Walmart's economics and competitive position in the early 1990s but didn't buy because the stock was a few points above his ideal price. He watched Google's dominance emerge through Berkshire's GEICO advertising spend — seeing firsthand the extraordinary return on investment of Google search ads — and still didn't invest. These are errors of omission, and Buffett considers them far more costly than Dexter or US Air.

<!-- p:1a3878 -->
Errors of omission are structurally different from errors of commission. They don't appear on any financial statement. No auditor flags them. No shareholder can point to a specific line item. Yet Buffett estimates that his errors of omission have cost Berkshire tens of billions of dollars — dwarfing any mistake that actually shows up in the accounts. This asymmetry reveals something important about how the investment profession keeps score: the visible loss from a bad investment receives intense scrutiny, while the invisible loss from a missed opportunity receives almost none.

<!-- p:96699b -->
> "You don't have to make a hundred correct judgments in this business or 50 correct judgments. You only have to make a few."

<!-- p:fd2178 -->
The mathematical structure of compounding means that missing even one or two transformative investments can reduce terminal wealth by orders of magnitude. A single correct decision on Walmart in 1992 or Google in 2004 — held for decades — would have generated more value than dozens of competent but unremarkable allocations. This is the cruel arithmetic of omission: the cost is invisible, deferred, and enormous.

<!-- p:c5a38f -->
## Precision Castparts and the Price of Impatience

<!-- p:7242ae -->
The 2016 acquisition of Precision Castparts for $37 billion represents a different category of error: correct thesis, wrong price. Buffett paid a premium for a business he admired — a dominant aerospace parts supplier with genuine pricing power and high switching costs. When aerospace demand softened and then collapsed during COVID, the acquisition required significant write-downs. Buffett acknowledged that he overpaid.

<!-- p:81faf0 -->
This mistake is particularly instructive because Buffett is famous for patience and discipline in pricing. The Precision Castparts error suggests that even the most disciplined investors can be seduced by business quality into paying too much. It also highlights a subtle form of overconfidence: believing that a wonderful business can overcome any starting valuation. As Buffett has noted in the context of Berkshire's early textile operations, he spent years trying to avoid repeating the mistake of deploying capital into businesses that couldn't earn adequate returns:

<!-- p:95eadb -->
> "We feel it is essential that we not repeat the history of the 1956-1966 period, when over-all earnings on average invested capital of over $30 million earned something less than zero."

<!-- p:49ffe9 -->
The Precision Castparts write-down brought this lesson full circle: even a great business can be a poor investment if the entry price embeds too much optimism.

<!-- p:e471b4 -->
## The Architecture of Survivable Mistakes

<!-- p:d0f950 -->
What distinguishes Buffett and Munger from investors who made similar errors but didn't survive is not the quality of their decisions but the structure around those decisions. Every major Berkshire mistake occurred within a framework that made it non-fatal.

<!-- p:3372f8 -->
> "We will always maintain supreme financial strength, operating with at least $20 billion of cash equivalents and never incurring material amounts of short-term debt."

<!-- p:a28d2a -->
This is not a platitude — it is the structural precondition that allows mistakes to remain mistakes rather than becoming catastrophes. The Great Depression destroyed sophisticated investors not because they were wrong about valuations but because leverage and illiquidity converted temporary impairment into permanent loss:

<!-- p:02af8a -->
> "What was unique and remarkable about the Great Depression, was that even the smart money got wiped out. The people who realized things are cheap now... were destroyed."

<!-- p:48bf46 -->
Berkshire's permanent conservatism — the $20 billion cash floor, the absence of material short-term debt, the diversified earnings streams from insurance, utilities, and operating businesses — creates a margin of error wide enough to absorb any single mistake. The same logic applies to Berkshire's insurance operations, where underwriting discipline serves as an error-absorption mechanism:

<!-- p:b02041 -->
> "If the insurance industry should experience a $250 billion loss from some mega-catastrophe... Berkshire as a whole would likely record a significant profit for the year."

<!-- p:0b6711 -->
The lesson is architectural: before worrying about which specific mistakes you might make, build a structure that can absorb the mistakes you will inevitably make. [[Margin_of_safety]] applies not just to individual security selection but to the design of the entire enterprise.

<!-- p:61df43 -->
## The Asymmetry of Learning from Failure

<!-- p:9cbc70 -->
Munger argued throughout his career that studying failure is more productive than studying success, for reasons rooted in psychology and probability. Success has many fathers — talent, timing, luck, leverage — and disentangling them is nearly impossible. Failure, by contrast, usually has identifiable, repeatable causes: overconfidence, excessive leverage, straying outside one's competence, ignoring adverse evidence, and compounding errors by refusing to reverse course.

<!-- p:fadf6e -->
> "People who do it two or three times early, all go broke because they think it's easy. In fact, it's very hard and rare."

<!-- p:d1693a -->
This observation points to a specific cognitive trap: survivorship-biased learning. Early success teaches investors that they are skilled, when they may simply be lucky. The asymmetry is that a few successes create dangerous overconfidence, while a few failures — properly analyzed — create durable wisdom. Munger's approach was to collect examples of catastrophic failure across domains — what he called an "anti-checklist" — and use them as a filter before making any commitment.

<!-- p:549e6b -->
The failure to account for second order effects compounds this problem. Munger noted that Medicare cost projections were wrong by over 1000% because analysts failed to consider how new incentives would change behavior:

<!-- p:6256ed -->
> "They chose not to consider effects of effects on effects, and so on."

<!-- p:3c8a33 -->
Investment mistakes follow the same pattern. Buying Dexter Shoe was not just wrong about shoes; it was wrong about globalization, about the fragility of domestic manufacturing moats, and about the compounding cost of paying in appreciated stock. Each of these was a second or third order effect that a simple "is this a good business?" analysis failed to capture.

<!-- p:f76c99 -->
## Implications for the Practitioner

<!-- p:656930 -->
The aggregate lesson from Buffett and Munger's errors is not to avoid mistakes — that is impossible — but to build a practice that makes mistakes survivable and educational. Several principles emerge:

<!-- p:3b11f2 -->
First, position sizing and financial conservatism must be structural, not situational. The time to build cash reserves is before the crisis, not during it. Second, the willingness to reverse a mistake quickly — to look foolish rather than go broke — is a competitive advantage that compounds over decades. Third, errors of omission deserve the same rigorous post-mortem as errors of commission, even though they are invisible in the accounting. Fourth, early success is more dangerous than early failure, because it teaches overconfidence rather than humility.

<!-- p:47fa40 -->
> "It isn't that we were so good at doing things that were difficult. We were good at avoiding things that were difficult — finding things that are easy."

<!-- p:2cbf86 -->
The deepest insight may be that Buffett and Munger's greatest skill was not analytical but temperamental: the ability to be ruthlessly honest about their own mistakes, to extract the maximum lesson from each one, and to build systems — financial, psychological, organizational — that converted inevitable human error into sustainable competitive advantage. The mistakes were real. The learning was the moat.
