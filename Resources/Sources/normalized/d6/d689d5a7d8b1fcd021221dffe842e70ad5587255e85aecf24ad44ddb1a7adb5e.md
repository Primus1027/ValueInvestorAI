# Intrinsic Value: Theory and Practice

<!-- p:a6b484 -->
The concept of intrinsic value sits at the foundation of all intelligent investing, yet it resists precise definition. Graham and Dodd's revolutionary contribution was separating price from value — the recognition that "the price of a stock at any given day, may or may not reflect the actual value" of the underlying business. What followed over the next seven decades was an evolving framework for estimating that value, one that moved from balance sheet arithmetic toward a richer understanding of earning power, capital allocation, and the compounding effects of retained earnings.

<!-- p:fef9df -->
## The Conceptual Foundation: DCF as Philosophy, Not Spreadsheet

<!-- p:39a557 -->
At its core, intrinsic value is the discounted present value of all future cash flows an owner will extract from a business. But Buffett has always treated this as a conceptual anchor rather than a computational exercise. The framework is deceptively simple: estimate the cash a business will generate over its remaining life, discount it back to the present, and compare to the asking price. The discipline lies not in the spreadsheet but in the judgment.

<!-- p:0c6f2d -->
Buffett uses at least a 10% discount rate for future after-tax cash streams, but critically adjusts based on business certainty:

<!-- p:45ed7c -->
> "The more certain we feel about a business, the closer we are willing to play it."

<!-- p:95a4c0 -->
This single sentence contains a profound insight that most valuation textbooks miss. The discount rate is not a fixed number derived from beta or CAPM — it is a reflection of the investor's confidence in the durability of cash flows. A business like See's Candy, with pricing power rooted in decades of consumer loyalty, warrants a very different discount rate than a commodity manufacturer subject to cyclical swings. discount rate is ultimately a measure of predictability, not market volatility.

<!-- p:852b1b -->
Munger and Buffett explicitly rejected academic finance's equation of risk with beta and volatility: "Risk = chance of going out of business." This redefinition matters enormously for valuation. If risk is not volatility but permanent capital loss, then a stock that has dropped 50% may actually be less risky than before — its [[margin_of_safety]] has widened, not narrowed.

<!-- p:ac7d4f -->
Buffett decomposes investment value into probability times magnitude. He willingly pays up for high-probability, moderate-magnitude outcomes but avoids low-probability, high-magnitude bets:

<!-- p:cbd8fd -->
> "Buffett is happy to pay for things with a modest value, but a high probability of it happening. But it's not his style at all to make bets on low probability, very high potential value plays."

<!-- p:e42289 -->
This probability-weighted approach explains why Buffett excels with stable, predictable businesses — he was, as one observer noted, "the world's greatest status quo investor." When the future largely resembles the present, the DCF framework works brilliantly. When it doesn't, Buffett simply declines to play.

<!-- p:3fae85 -->
## Owner Earnings vs. Reported Earnings: What Accounting Conceals

<!-- p:965f64 -->
Perhaps no gap in investing is wider than the one between reported earnings and true economic earnings. Buffett's concept of owner earnings — net income plus depreciation and amortization, minus required capital expenditures — cuts through accounting conventions to reveal what an owner actually takes home.

<!-- p:f45cb9 -->
Munger framed the accounting problem with characteristic bluntness:

<!-- p:f9a31f -->
> "You have to know enough about it to understand its limitations — because although accounting is the starting place, it's only a crude approximation. And it's not very hard to understand its limitations."

<!-- p:779657 -->
The limitations run in both directions. Accounting can overstate economic reality, as with Berkshire's textile operations, which over six years "generated essentially zero net earnings ($40,476 total) while requiring heavy capital expenditures." A business that appears profitable in good years but consumes capital through full cycles is not truly earning anything — the profits are illusory, devoured by the reinvestment treadmill.

<!-- p:fb4267 -->
Munger's experience with a Caterpillar tractor dealership crystallized this:

<!-- p:d8d07f -->
> "He looks at his used machines, takes it in as customers buy the new ones and says, 'There's all my profit, rusting in my yard.' We hate that kind of business."

<!-- p:f1ced4 -->
This is the dark side of capital intensity — earnings that never convert to cash because the business demands constant reinvestment simply to maintain its competitive position. capital intensity is the silent destroyer of intrinsic value.

<!-- p:a8d7ce -->
But accounting also systematically understates economic reality. Berkshire's equity portfolio provided a case study for decades. Of $4.4 billion in Berkshire's share of its Big Four investees' earnings, only $1.4 billion appeared in reported results. The retained $3 billion compounded silently:

<!-- p:8d1a07 -->
> "Make no mistake: The $3 billion of their earnings we don't report is every bit as valuable to us as the portion Berkshire records."

<!-- p:64cf1a -->
This insight — that retained earnings working inside investee companies are just as real as dividends received — is the engine behind long-term equity returns. Edgar Lawrence Smith identified this mechanism decades earlier, earning Keynes's endorsement: well-managed companies retain earnings and reinvest them, creating a compound interest effect that steadily builds intrinsic value. The GAAP income statement, which only records dividends received from non-controlled holdings, renders this entire compounding machine invisible.

## The EBITDA Illusion and What Depreciation Really Means

<!-- p:2476e5 -->
Buffett's most withering criticism has been reserved for Wall Street's favorite metric: adjusted EBITDA. His invocation of Lincoln was pointed:

<!-- p:8a7a88 -->
> "Abraham Lincoln once posed the question: 'If you call a dog's tail a leg, how many legs does it have?' and then answered his own query: 'Four, because calling a tail a leg doesn't make it one.' Abe would have felt lonely on Wall Street."

<!-- p:93e1d8 -->
The EBITDA framework treats depreciation as a non-cash add-back, implying that capital expenditures are somehow optional. For asset-light businesses, this distortion is minor. For capital-intensive businesses — airlines, telecom, manufacturing — it is catastrophic. Depreciation represents real economic wear, and the capital expenditures required to replace aging assets are as mandatory as paying employees.

<!-- p:8450cb -->
Yet Buffett draws a crucial distinction often missed by his critics. Acquisition-related amortization of intangibles is genuinely not an economic cost — a purchased brand does not depreciate the way a machine does. Meanwhile, depreciation of physical assets often *understates* true capital needs, especially during inflationary periods. The investor who adds back all D&A and calls the result "earnings" is fooling himself in the most dangerous direction.

<!-- p:24ec8c -->
The practical consequence is that [[free_cash_flow]] — not EBITDA, not net income — is the closest approximation to what a private owner would actually pocket. When Buffett evaluated See's Candy, the decisive metric was $4 million in pre-tax profit "growing at 12% per year without putting any more capital into the business." The words "without putting any more capital in" are doing all the analytical work. See's required no incremental investment to grow — its owner earnings equaled or exceeded its reported earnings.

<!-- p:123484 -->
Contrast this with Berkshire's textile operations, where reported earnings were periodically healthy but required "heavy capital expenditures" to sustain. The difference between these two businesses — one that converts earnings to cash and one that converts cash to earnings — is the difference between genuine and illusory intrinsic value.

<!-- p:3e65e6 -->
## Why Precision Is the Enemy of Accuracy

<!-- p:5343a9 -->
A recurring theme across decades of Buffett and Munger commentary is the rejection of false precision in valuation. Munger noted that "reported numbers like depreciation rates create an illusion of precision where only rough guesses exist." The entire edifice of discounted cash flow analysis rests on estimates of future cash flows and appropriate discount rates — neither of which can be known with precision.

<!-- p:ef5c4f -->
This is why Buffett's valuation framework is better understood as a filter than a calculator. When offered a business for $6 million that had $2 million in cash, $2 million in real estate, and earned $2 million pre-tax annually, the correct response was immediate action:

<!-- p:0e8940 -->
> "The trick when you get in business with somebody who wants to sell you something for $6 million that's got $2 million of cash, a couple million of real estate, and is making $2 million a year, is you don't want to be patient at that moment."

<!-- p:aa710a -->
No DCF model was needed. The value was obvious to "an orangutan," as Munger said of Washington Post trading at one-fifth of its plain value. The two-minute valuation works precisely because it demands such a wide [[margin_of_safety]] that small errors in estimation become irrelevant.

<!-- p:0f8cd5 -->
Berkshire's Apple position illustrates this principle at scale. The purchase was not a technology thesis but a valuation observation:

<!-- p:115b3b -->
> "It got cheap. It got to about 10 times earnings when Warren bought in."

<!-- p:7d5789 -->
A dominant company with a massive installed base, extraordinary brand loyalty, and growing services revenue at 10x earnings requires no elaborate model. The margin of safety is self-evident. This is what Buffett means when he says he'd rather be approximately right than precisely wrong.

<!-- p:3c3a81 -->
The corollary is equally important: when valuation requires precision to justify a purchase, the opportunity is probably not attractive enough. If you need a spreadsheet to prove the margin of safety exists, it probably doesn't.

## Growth as a Component of Value, Not Its Substitute

<!-- p:f5e8f4 -->
One of the most misunderstood aspects of value investing is the relationship between growth and value. They are not opposites — growth is a component of the intrinsic value calculation, sometimes adding value and sometimes destroying it.

<!-- p:689094 -->
Buffett would consider buying a business with no current cash flow if the future earning power was compelling:

<!-- p:7b054a -->
> "If we think the present value of the future earning power is attractive enough compared to the purchase price, we would not be overwhelmed by what the first year's figure would be."

<!-- p:c7a0a5 -->
He demonstrated this conviction by buying GEICO "at a time when it was losing significant money," looking past current results to the earning power that would emerge once the business stabilized. This is growth investing in its purest form — not paying for momentum, but recognizing suppressed earning power.

<!-- p:f0f545 -->
But growth only creates value when the business earns attractive returns on the capital reinvested. Berkshire's five-pillar value creation framework makes this explicit: improving subsidiary earning power, bolt-on acquisitions, investee growth, share repurchases below intrinsic value, and occasional large acquisitions — "while rarely issuing shares." Each pillar requires that incremental capital deployed earns returns above its cost. Growth that consumes capital at sub-par returns destroys intrinsic value even as it inflates reported earnings.

<!-- p:0c3b4a -->
This is why Buffett evaluates acquisitions on an all-equity basis, refusing to let financial engineering mask poor economics:

<!-- p:8810a5 -->
> "Even a high-priced deal will usually boost per-share earnings if it is debt-financed. At Berkshire, in contrast, we evaluate acquisitions on an all-equity basis."

<!-- p:3ee7bd -->
The abundance of cheap debt creates an illusion of value creation. A mediocre business acquired at a high price can appear accretive on an earnings-per-share basis simply because debt is cheap. Strip away the leverage, and the true economic return reveals itself.

<!-- p:ed5e25 -->
## The Balance Sheet as Truth Serum

<!-- p:d8c248 -->
While Wall Street fixates on quarterly earnings, Buffett reveals a contrarian practice:

<!-- p:bbe5b8 -->
> "I spend more time looking at balance sheets than I do income statements. Wall Street doesn't pay much attention to balance sheets, but I like to look at balance sheets over an 8 or 10 year period before I even look at the income account because there are certain things it's harder to hide or play games with on the balance sheet."

<!-- p:87f81d -->
This preference reveals a deep insight about where intrinsic value hides and where accounting gamesmanship flourishes. Income statements are the domain of management creativity — revenue recognition timing, expense capitalization, non-GAAP adjustments. Balance sheets, examined over a decade, expose the accumulation or erosion of real economic value: whether cash is building or debt is growing, whether working capital is healthy, whether asset values are supported by earning power.

<!-- p:949703 -->
Ironically, even balance sheets have limitations that cut against the investor. Berkshire's insurance float, carried as a full liability, dramatically understates intrinsic value:

<!-- p:b5c799 -->
> "But to think of float as strictly a liability is incorrect; it should instead be viewed as a revolving fund."

<!-- p:ea2ea7 -->
Float that is costless and perpetually replenished has an economic value far below its accounting liability. Similarly, operating businesses carried at historical cost — never marked up regardless of value creation — make book value an increasingly conservative measure:

<!-- p:074278 -->
> "Many of these are worth far more than their cost-based carrying value. But that amount is never revalued upward no matter how much the value of these companies has increased."

<!-- p:5e6672 -->
The gap between book value and intrinsic value has widened so much that Buffett abandoned his decades-long practice of leading annual letters with book value changes. The accounting scorecard had become "increasingly out of touch with economic reality."

<!-- p:e53a9e -->
For the practitioner, the lesson is to treat both income statements and balance sheets as starting points — useful but incomplete maps of economic reality. True intrinsic value lives in the cash flows a business generates, the capital it requires, and the durability of its [[competitive_moat]]. Everything else is approximation.
